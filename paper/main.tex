\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Selection systems that admit more entrants are widely assumed to be fairer, but breadth can
silently cost \emph{strategyproofness} --- the property that a competitor never gains by
underperforming. We study the 2026 expansion of the FIFA World Cup from 32 to 48 teams, whose
group stage advances, besides the top two of each group, the eight best third-placed teams. We
define a \emph{manipulable match-state} as one in which a team's advancement-optimal action is not
to maximise its chance of winning, and estimate its prevalence by Monte-Carlo simulation of the
full tournament under calibrated team-strength models. Final-matchday manipulability is markedly
higher under the 48-team format: the expansion multiplier is
$\rho_{48}/\rho_{32}=1.66$ (95\% CI $[1.59,1.73]$). A factorial decomposition shows this is caused
almost entirely by the best-third rule ($1.72\times$, holding field, groups and draw fixed), not by
the larger field or group count ($0.96\times$, indistinguishable from no effect). The rule uniquely
creates \emph{cross-group} manipulability --- incentives that route through other groups' results and
therefore survive the simultaneous final kickoffs FIFA introduced after the 1982 ``Disgrace of
Gij\'on'' --- present in $48\%$ of manipulable 48-team states and $0\%$ under 32 teams. A restricted
theorem explains the mechanism: group qualification is monotone in own performance (no
threshold-tanking), so manipulability is path-valued, and the best-third rule injects a cross-group
advancement channel that within-group simultaneity cannot neutralise. We pre-register, before the
group finale is played, the specific 2026 matches our model flags as manipulable. The price of
expansion is a quantifiable, mechanistically identified increase in manipulability.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}
Whether a competition is \emph{strategyproof} --- whether a participant can ever gain by not
trying to win --- is a mechanism-design property with a long lineage in social choice, where the
Gibbard--Satterthwaite theorem \cite{gibbard1973,satterthwaite1975} shows that non-trivial choice
rules are generically manipulable. Tournament design is the sporting instance of the same question
\cite{csato2021tournament,kendall2017sportsrules}, and its failures are concrete: at the 1982 World
Cup in Gij\'on, West Germany and Austria played out a result that eliminated Algeria, after which
FIFA mandated that a group's final two matches kick off \emph{simultaneously} so that neither team
can play to a known target. Simultaneity removes manipulation that depends on a contemporaneously
played match \emph{within a team's own group}.

The 2026 expansion to 48 teams changes the advancement rule, not merely its scale: twelve groups of
four send their top two plus the eight best third-placed teams to a Round of 32. A third-placed
team's fate is decided by comparison with teams in \emph{other} groups --- a cross-group dependency
that within-group simultaneity cannot conceal. Existing analyses of the expansion treat
competitiveness and balance \cite{csato2025imbalanced} or forecast outcomes; the strategyproofness
of the new format, and a clean attribution of any change to the rule rather than the larger field,
have not been established. Incentive incompatibility of qualification more broadly is known
\cite{dagaev2018winning,pauly2014roundrobin,vong2017strategic,guyon2015rethinking}, but as
existence, not as a measured, mechanistically decomposed, and prospectively tested quantity.

We make three contributions. First, we define and measure the prevalence of manipulable
final-matchday states under both formats. Second, we causally isolate the best-third rule by a
factorial that holds the field, group structure and draw fixed, and prove a restricted theorem that
explains why the rule, and not the expansion per se, creates manipulability that simultaneity cannot
remove. Third, we pre-register the specific 2026 matches our model flags, turning the tournament
into a prospective natural experiment rather than a retrospective fit.

\section*{Results}
\subsection*{Manipulable match-states and the expansion multiplier}
We simulate each format's full bracket under two independent team-strength models (Methods), and at
every team's final-group-match decision compare the action that maximises its probability of winning
the match with the action that maximises its advancement value $V_{\mathrm{adv}}$ (expected knockout
rounds won). A state is manipulable when these differ (Methods). Across the official 2026 draw, the
final-matchday manipulability rate is $\rho_{48}=0.26$ versus $\rho_{32}=0.16$, an expansion
multiplier of $\rho_{48}/\rho_{32}=1.66$ (95\% CI $[1.59,1.73]$; $400$ snapshots $\times$ $150$ inner,
cluster bootstrap over Monte-Carlo snapshots). The interval excludes 1 across model families,
manipulability margins, and Monte-Carlo budgets (Robustness; Fig.~\ref{fig:rates}a).

\subsection*{The cause is the rule, not the field}
The multiplier conflates several simultaneous changes (field size, group count, advancement rule,
draw). We isolate the best-third rule with a three-arm factorial: (A) the real 48-team format; (B)
the identical 48-team field, groups and draw but with only the top two advancing; and (C) the 32-team
baseline. The multiplier decomposes multiplicatively as $\rho_A/\rho_C=(\rho_A/\rho_B)(\rho_B/\rho_C)$.
The best-third-rule term is $\rho_A/\rho_B=1.72$ (95\% CI $[1.66,1.79]$), while the field-plus-group
term is $\rho_B/\rho_C=0.96$ (95\% CI $[0.92,1.01]$), statistically indistinguishable from no effect.
Cross-group manipulability --- states whose advancement-optimal action routes through the best-third
pool --- accounts for $48\%$ of manipulable states under the rule and is identically $0\%$ without it
(arms B and C). Expanding the field without the wildcard rule produces essentially no additional, and
no cross-group, manipulability (Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}).

\subsection*{A restricted theorem}
Two propositions explain the simulation (Methods; full statements and proofs in the Supplementary
Theory). \emph{Proposition 1:} holding others' results fixed, the set of a team's own results for
which it advances is upward-closed in $\{\mathrm{LOSE}\prec\mathrm{DRAW}\prec\mathrm{WIN}\}$ in both
formats; hence winning maximises the probability of advancing and there is no pure-qualification
manipulation. All manipulability is therefore path/seeding-valued --- consistent with the simulated
qualification-objective rate being $\approx 0$ in both formats. \emph{Proposition 2:} the best-third
rule creates states in which a team's advancement-optimal action is not to win and the deciding
dependency is cross-group --- it still qualifies as a best third (a comparison across groups) and a
lower finish routes it, via the fixed Round-of-32 assignment, to a weaker path. Because the deciding
quantities lie outside the team's group, within-group simultaneity cannot remove the incentive. No
state of this type exists under 32 teams, where third place is elimination. We do not claim the
32-team format is manipulation-free --- seeding manipulation exists at every breadth --- only that
the best-third rule adds a cross-group channel; the magnitude is the empirical decomposition above.

\subsection*{Robustness}
The primary estimate ($1.66$) is stable across analytic choices: the named-prediction ranking is
preserved across the two strength-model families (rank correlation $\rho_s=0.96$) and across three
manipulability margins (rank correlation $\approx0.98$), and a separate convergence study shows the
multiplier is stable once the Monte-Carlo budget exceeds $\approx 80$ snapshots, motivating the
$400$-snapshot budget used throughout (Fig.~\ref{fig:conv}); every 95\% interval excludes 1.
Confidence intervals use a cluster bootstrap that resamples whole snapshots, the correct unit given
within-snapshot correlation. Under alternative advancement values --- probability
of qualifying, and probability of winning the title --- the qualification objective yields no
manipulability in either format (Proposition 1) and the title objective is too sparse to estimate
reliably; the canonical rounds-won objective is reported throughout.

\subsection*{Pre-registered live test on the 2026 group finale}
After Matchday~1 and before Matchday~2, we register the matches our model flags as containing a
manipulable team, ranked by probability and labelled by whether the incentive is cross-group
(Methods; the frozen artifact and protocol are version-controlled and time-stamped). We distinguish
two kinds of claim. The expansion multiplier ($\rho_{48}/\rho_{32}>1$) and the cross-group share are
\emph{computational estimands} --- properties of the format, the draw and the strength model that are
fixed by the frozen computation rather than tested against play (and the $32$-team cross-group share
is zero by construction, not an empirical finding). The genuinely \emph{prospective} object is the
named Matchday-3 prediction set, evaluated after the finale by a secondary, explicitly confoundable
analysis comparing implicated teams' observed passivity against a strength-matched historical
baseline. The frozen predictions are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:pred}: five matches clear the robust threshold under both models (Brazil--Scotland,
Canada--Switzerland, Czech~Republic--Mexico, Algeria--Austria, Netherlands--Sweden), with
Algeria--Austria and the Group~F matches carrying the largest cross-group component. By the
pre-registered power rule the expected number of realised robust manipulable matches is $2.76<3$, so
the behavioural endpoint is reported as illustrative; the computational estimands (the multiplier and
the cross-group share) are the paper's quantitative core, and the named predictions are the prospective
falsifiable claim.

\emph{Outcome (group stage complete).} Scoring the frozen predictions against the realised standings,
\textbf{four of the five} registered robust matches presented a manipulation incentive at their true
Matchday-3 decision point; the two strongest cross-group calls were near-certain in hindsight ---
Canada--Switzerland ($99\%$) and Algeria--Austria ($96\%$, $96\%$ cross-group), the latter ending in a
high-scoring $3$--$3$ draw that advanced both teams (the second via the best-third pool). The model's
frozen ranking correlated with the realised incentive structure (Spearman $\approx0.46$); the highest
realised cross-group incentives (Algeria--Austria, Japan--Tunisia, Curaçao--Ecuador) were all driven
by the best-third channel. Consistent with the power rule and the absence of event-level data, we
report that the \emph{structural incentive was present and correctly anticipated}, not that teams
demonstrably acted on it (the $3$--$3$ is suggestive but confoundable).

\section*{Discussion}
Our results separate two things usually conflated in debates over expanding a competition: admitting
more entrants, and the rule used to admit them. The manipulability cost of the 2026 World Cup is not
a price of size; it is a price of the specific best-third wildcard, which makes advancement depend on
other groups in a way the post-Gij\'on simultaneity fix cannot reach. The same logic applies to any
selection system that admits a marginal tier by cross-comparing otherwise-separate competitions ---
wild-card play-offs, cross-panel grant or hiring quotas, multi-region qualification --- where the
remedy is structural (synchronise or sever the cross-comparison), not exhortatory. The principal
limitation is that our behavioural test of manipulation is confoundable by rotation, fatigue and
tactics; we therefore make the behaviour-independent manipulable-state count the primary claim and
pre-register it. Remaining modelling extensions --- a finer action space capturing within-win
scoreline targeting, an equilibrium (rather than best-response) treatment of the simultaneous finale,
and goal-model calibration with the Dixon--Coles low-score correction \cite{dixoncoles1997} --- are
expected to sharpen, not overturn, the identified mechanism.

\section*{Methods}
\paragraph{Data and strength models.} We fit two independent models to historical international
results: a time-varying Elo with an Elo-to-Poisson scoreline sampler, and a hierarchical Bayesian
Poisson (attack/defence with home advantage, non-centred, sampled by NUTS with convergence verified
for the 48 finalists). Probabilities are assessed by proper scores (Brier, log-loss, ranked
probability score) \cite{gneiting2007scoring} rather than accuracy. The current Poisson is
independent (no Dixon--Coles low-score correlation); calibration and a corrected refit are reported
as robustness.
\paragraph{Simulator and bracket.} A Monte-Carlo engine plays the group stage under FIFA ranking
criteria (points, goal difference, goals scored, head-to-head) and the official 2026
Round-of-32$\rightarrow$final bracket, including the candidate-set assignment of the eight best
third-placed teams to bracket slots. Format and draw are the swapped variables.
\paragraph{Manipulable states and $V_{\mathrm{adv}}$.} For a team's final-group match we condition
the scoreline on each target result $\{\mathrm{WIN},\mathrm{DRAW},\mathrm{LOSE}\}$, simulate the
remainder against the fixed strength model (a decision-theoretic best response, not an equilibrium),
and set $V_{\mathrm{adv}}$ to expected knockout rounds won (zero if eliminated; this subsumes
advancement probability and bracket-path strength). A state is manipulable when the
$V_{\mathrm{adv}}$-maximising action is not WIN by more than a noise margin; it is cross-group when
the advancement-optimal action qualifies the team via the best-third pool. The remainder of the
tournament --- including every other group's final matchday and the team's own group's other final
match --- is \emph{integrated over by Monte-Carlo, not observed}: the team chooses its action under
the same simultaneous, no-cross-group-information set that the post-Gij\'on fix is designed to
enforce. The reported cross-group share is therefore the conservative, fully-simultaneous bound: a
team is flagged only when its advancement-optimal action beats WIN while averaging over other groups'
unknown results. The real staggered Matchday-3 schedule, under which some groups' finales are already
decided at a later team's kickoff, relaxes this information set and can only make such incentives
sharper; we treat the simultaneous bound as the headline and quantify the staggered regime as an
extension. Estimands, the cluster bootstrap, the convergence-justified budget, and the full
pre-registration protocol are given in the Supplementary Information.
\paragraph{Pre-registration.} The model, predictions and scoring rule were fixed and time-stamped
before the group finale; the protocol, frozen prediction artifact and analysis code are public.
